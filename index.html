The Basics

Geography

Where is it?

Gambia sits in West Africa and is completely surrounded by Senegal on all sides except the Atlantic coast. It is the smallest country on the African mainland — basically a thin strip of land on both sides of the Gambian River.

CapitalBanjul
Largest citySerekunda
IndependenceFrom UK, 1965
River & Coast

The Gambian River & Coast

The country literally follows the river — it's why Gambia is shaped like a long thin strip. The river is critical for fishing, growing crops (water for irrigation), and trading by boat — easy to ship goods along it. Historically it was also used to transport enslaved people to the coast.

Where the river meets the ocean it forms a delta — swamps and mangroves with rich wildlife (birds, fish). The Atlantic coast has sandy beaches which attract tourists.

The Harmattan Wind

In the dry season, a hot dry wind called the Harmattan blows in from the Sahara desert, carrying huge clouds of dust across the country. This can reduce visibility and dry out the land even further.

Climate & Landscape

Climate Aw

Tropical Savannah

Gambia has a tropical savannah climate (Aw). This means it is always above 18°C — it never gets cold. There are two seasons:

Rainy seasonJune – October
Dry seasonRest of the year

In the dry season, winds sometimes blow in huge clouds of dust from the Sahara.

Vegetation

Two Types of Savannah

Park savannahDry · north of river
Forest savannahLess dry · south of river

There is less forest savannah now because of deforestation. This creates a dangerous cycle:

deforestation→ less vegetation→ less water vapour→ drier climate→ trees die

People & Society

Population

Demographics

Population~3 million
Density180/km²
Average ageUnder 25 years
Children/woman4 (average)

Gambia is the most densely populated African country. Population is growing fast and skews very young.

Culture

Ethnic Groups & Religion

There are about 20 ethnic groups. The two biggest are the Mandinka and the Fula. The Fula are traditionally nomadic — they move around with their livestock rather than settling in one place.

90% of people are Muslim — Islam is central to daily life and culture. Nature worshipping and traditional animist practices are forbidden under Islamic law.

Almost everyone speaks English (colonial legacy from British rule).

Settlement

Where People Live

People prefer to live near the river or coast because:

  • Water to grow crops (agriculture)
  • Easy to ship goods by boat
  • Tourism is higher near the coast

Most poverty is in rural areas away from the river.

Development & HDI

HDI explained

What is HDI and why does it matter?

HDI stands for Human Development Index. It measures how developed a country is — not just by money, but by the quality of life of its people. It looks at things like food security, clean water access, teachers per child, and life expectancy.

This is important because income alone doesn't tell the full story. A person might earn $5/day in Gambia and $50/day in the Netherlands — but $5 in Gambia doesn't buy the same things as $5 in the Netherlands. Income ≠ purchasing power, because prices are different in every country.

GDP (Gross Domestic Product) = the total money a country makes from all its goods and services in a year. It's useful but imperfect — it measures country wealth, not individual people's wealth. A country can have high GDP while most citizens are still very poor.
Health

Health Stats (Gambia vs Netherlands)

Life expectancy65 yrs  vs  82 yrs (NL)
Infant death rate62‰  vs  3.6‰ (NL)
Doctors1 per 15,000 patients

The infant death rate is almost 17× higher than the Netherlands. Access to basic healthcare is severely limited — and corruption makes it worse (e.g. the government blocks medicine shipments coming in from other countries like the Netherlands).

Education

Why Education Is Failing

Gambia has no compulsory education up to age 18. Very few children reach higher education. Why?

  • Too expensive for most families
  • Kids are needed on the farm to feed the family
  • No money = no school = stuck in agriculture = no money
This is a poverty trap — low education keeps people in low-income farming, which keeps them too poor to afford education.

Economy

Labour force

The 3-Sector Model

Every country's workforce can be split into 3 sectors. In general: less agriculture = more industry = more money. The Netherlands is almost entirely services. Gambia is the opposite.

SectorGambiaNetherlands
Agriculture75%~2%
Industry19%~17%
Services6%~80%
Exports

Peanuts & Monoculture Risk

Gambia mainly exports peanuts and peanut products. Also: fishing, tourism.

The big problem: this is monoculture — relying on one crop. This is extremely risky because:

  • If peanut prices drop globally → no income
  • If the harvest fails → famine and starvation
  • No profit being made from exports currently

Most farmers are subsistence farmers — they eat their own crops rather than selling them. They're poor, not exporting much.

World system

Where Does Gambia Fit?

The world is divided into three tiers:

1st world2nd world3rd world
Core countrySemi-peripheryPeriphery ← Gambia

Core countries (like NL) are wealthy, industrialised, and powerful. Periphery countries like Gambia are poor, dependent on agriculture, and economically exploited by or dependent on core countries.

Basic Needs & Poverty

Poverty

Why Basic Needs Matter

The basic needs for human life are: food, water, shelter, healthcare, and education. If a country cannot provide these affordably, everyone lives in poverty — regardless of what the GDP number says.

People in poverty50%
Poverty locationMostly rural
People reaching age 70Only 2%

Gambia is one of the poorest countries in the world. Corruption makes it worse — for example, the government has blocked medicine shipments coming in from other countries.

Colonial History

1400s–1965

From Trade to Slavery to Independence

Around 1400–1500, Europeans arrived in West Africa mainly for trade. They exchanged gold, ivory, pepper — and eventually people. Within about 100 years, the transatlantic slave trade had begun. Europeans enslaved Africans and shipped them to America — many through the Gambian River. Around 12 million people were transported as slaves.

The local governments of Gambia and Senegal tried to merge into a single country called "Senegambia" — but they never agreed on anything and it never happened. The current borders were formed around 1900 and follow the shape of the river. Gambia became independent from Britain in 1965.

The reason Gambia has such a strange shape — a thin strip surrounded by Senegal — is because the colonial borders were drawn following the Gambian River. Britain controlled the river; France controlled everything else (Senegal).

How Do You Prove Gambia Is Poor?

Evidence of poverty

What HDI Actually Looks Like in Gambia

HDI doesn't just tell you a number — it's proven by what you can actually see and measure. Here's how you know Gambia is poor without just saying "it's poor":

Visible signs

  • Children are visibly malnourished — skinny kids are a direct sign that basic food needs aren't being met
  • Schools look bad — crumbling buildings, no supplies, overcrowded classrooms
  • Gambians earn on average €1,400 per year — less than €4 per day
  • A country lives in poverty when its people cannot afford the basic needs (food, water, shelter, healthcare, education)

Measured indicators (HDI)

  • Life expectancy: 65 years (NL: 82)
  • Infant death rate: 62‰
  • Only 1 doctor per 15,000 patients
  • Very few teachers per child
  • Limited clean water access
  • 50% live below the poverty line
On a test: if asked "how can you tell Gambia is poor?", use both visible evidence (malnourished children, poor schools) AND measured statistics (life expectancy, infant death rate, income). That's what HDI captures.

Population & Settlement

Population pyramid

Wide at the Bottom

Gambia's population pyramid is wide at the bottom and narrow at the top. This means:

  • Huge numbers of young people (average age under 25)
  • Very few elderly — most don't survive to old age
  • High birth rate (4 children per woman) keeps the base wide
  • High death rate at older ages keeps the top narrow

This shape is typical of poor developing countries. Rich countries like the Netherlands have a more rectangular or top-heavy pyramid.

Where people live

Most People Live at the Delta

The majority of Gambians are concentrated near the river delta and coast — around Banjul and Serekunda. Reasons:

  • Fertile land for growing crops near water
  • River and coast make shipping easy
  • Tourism jobs concentrated on the coast
  • Sandy beaches attract visitors → income

The further inland (away from the river), the poorer and more sparsely populated it gets.
